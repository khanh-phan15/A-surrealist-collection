Salvador Dalí’s The Accommodations of Desire (1929) is a hallmark of Surrealist eroticism, with Freudian symbols like ants representing Dalí’s overwhelming sexual desire. The painting is a prime example of Dalí’s exploration of the unconscious mind, combining hyperrealistic detail with bizarre, dreamlike scenarios.

Dalí’s paintings, known for their hallucinatory quality, expanded on the dream imagery seen in works by René Magritte. His highly personal take on Freudian theory and the use of bizarre visual motifs pushed Surrealism into new realms of psychological exploration. Dalí's works continue to be some of the most iconic representations of the movement's obsession with desire and the subconscious.
